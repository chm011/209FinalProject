Diabetes Health Indicators

Can we predict your risk of diabetes simply by analyzing a few health indicators?

Diabetes is a chronic disease that impacts millions worldwide, characterized by hyperglycemia or high blood sugar. Typically, diabetes is diagnosed when HbA1c levels exceed 6.5% or blood sugar levels surpass 200 mg/dL on multiple readings. In 2019, over 133 million Americans were living with diabetes or prediabetes. Among them, approximately 37.3 million, or 11.3% of the U.S. population, had diabetes, with over one in four individuals over 65 affected. Alarmingly, nearly one in four adults with diabetes are unaware of their condition. Around 90% to 95% of diabetes cases are type 2 diabetes, typically linked to lifestyle factors. In 2019, roughly 96 million adults, or 38% of U.S. adults, were diagnosed with prediabetes, underscoring the importance of understanding risk factors for type 2 diabetes.

Since 2022, diabetes prevalence among adults aged 18 and older has reached 14%, a notable increase from 7% in 1990. Despite the rising number of cases, more than half (59%) of adults aged 30 and over with diabetes were not taking medication in 2022, with particularly low treatment coverage in low- and middle-income countries. Diabetes was the direct cause of 1.6 million deaths in 2021, nearly half of which (47%) occurred before the age of 70. Additionally, diabetes contributed to 530,000 deaths from kidney disease and accounted for about 11% of cardiovascular deaths. Since 2000, mortality rates from diabetes have increased, in contrast to a 20% decline in the probability of dying from the four major non-communicable diseases (cardiovascular disease, cancer, chronic respiratory disease, and diabetes) between 2000 and 2019. These statistics highlight the growing impact of diabetes and the need for effective prevention strategies.

To gain insights into key factors contributing to diabetes risk, I analyzed a diabetes dataset from the UCI repository, originally sourced from Kaggle. My goal was to explore the insights this data provides and assess the accuracy of analytical results compared to real-world statistics. Below, I describe two visualizations I created to better understand these factors and their relationships to diabetes.

Data Soruce (from 2014): https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/alexteboul/diabetes-health-indicators-dataset/data?select=diabetes_012_health_indicators_BRFSS2015.csv




Diabetes Prevalence by General Demographic

This first visualization shows the diabetes prevalence rate within the dataset, which is based on 70,692 survey responses. It provides an overview of the demographics related to age, sex, income, and education, along with the distribution of these demographic factors. Age and sex for general insight are included, as well as income and education, which, while less impactful in a clinical setting compared to lifestyle and physical health factors, are still worth exploring. The visualization allows viewers to filter by demographic category and hover over the bars to see the exact counts of people with or without diabetes in each subgroup, along with the total population in each subgroup.







Diabetes Risk Calculator

This calculator was made based on a kaggle set. It is meant to give an idea based on the data, and its prevalence rate and risk calculated are not meant to be extrapolated, but only to understand the risk further.

I processed the data to determine prevalence rates for each category and subgroup, which I used to create a scoring system. Each prevalence rate was assigned a risk score using predefined thresholds, and I calculated correlation coefficients to determine each factor's influence. These coefficients were used to weight the risk scores, which were summed, averaged, and converted into a percentage to provide an overall risk score for diabetes.

These visualizations aim to help us better understand factors influencing diabetes prevalence and risk, which is crucial in addressing this growing health issue.

    Risk Factors for Type 2 Diabetes
    Your chances of developing type 2 diabetes depend on a combination of risk factors. Risk factors related to family history, age, race, or ethnicity can't be changed; however, lifestyle changes are the best way to prevent or delay the onset diabetes. You may be able to avoid or delay diabetes just by maintaining a healthier weight and being physically active. Below are some of the risk factors deem more likely than others:

  • overweight or have obesity
  • have a familiy history of diabetes
  • are descendants of African American, American Indian, Asian American, Hispanic/Latino, or Pacific Islander
  • are physically inactive
  • have had gestational diabetes or prediabetes
